_"Rip and tear. Until it is done."_

 With those words, Robert Wilson (usually just referred to as Wilson) came out of the BD he had been scrolling. Setting his BD wreath aside, he took a moment to just sit there. Ride out the aftershocks of such gratuitous violence directed at the subclass of people of the city that deserved it the most.

 The BD itself had been excellent, both in content as well as the editing done to it. However amidst the killing spree that Ghost had so carefully scrolled there had been something that had stood out to him. Something that, akin to a speck of dust in your eye or a needle jabbed between your ribs, he simply could not ignore and that made him grind his teeth in frustration.

 The Carnage. That _fucking gun._

 You see, Wilson was something of an oddity in this day and age. An anomaly. In a world where life was cheap and guns were _even cheaper,_ in a city where violence was the norm, where nobody batted an eye at hearing gun shots or seeing someone bleed out in the street. Where gangs ruled the streets nearly unchecked and anyone could buy a single-use, fully automatic firearm from a vending machine.

 In such a world, in such a city, he alone was a man who above else valued _quality._

 And not the kind of quality you might find in high-spec manufacturers like MiliTech and Arasaka, either. This was not the quality borne from thousands upon thousands of wage-slaves working long hours in a sweatshop churning out identical piece after identical piece, no.

 Robustness. Reliability. Ergonomics. These were to him words most holy. Words that he strived to apply to every single weapon that passed through his hands, even at the cost of himself and his business.

 None of which applied to the Budget Arms Carnage.

 Oh how he loathed that gun. Hated it with such a passion that even now, flush with the endorphins of having watched such a sublime BD, he was all but shaking in rage.

 No. This would not do.

 Getting to his feet he was briefly struck with a strange sense of wrongness, of his body not moving quite right. However instead of distracting him it only reinforced his desire; his _need_ to make a gun that would not detract from the skill and ferocity that Ghost displayed, but enhance it.

 First order of business? Material and design, in that order.

 Most guns nowadays were made from various polymers, cheap to produce yet reasonably robust, but to him that was not enough.

 It would have to be strong enough to handle the stresses of firing again and again, strong enough to be able to take being dropped or thrown along with its user. Strong enough to be used as a shield to ward off attacks aimed to kill or cripple its wielder, as well as be used as a club when push came to shove.

 His course took him past the stacks upon stacks of high-tensile strength alloys and military-grade polymers that he kept in the back room. Though each having been originally purchased for the sake of a single weapon, the scraps left over often found their use in later projects. Waste not, want not.

 Normally these would be enough for your average person who just wanted a piece to shoot with, but not this time.

 At the very back of the room was a small secondary area normally reserved for keeping various essentials, cases of food and water in case your store came under siege. That sort of stuff. And although he _did_ indeed keep a few boxes of bottled water and nonperishables in there, his target this time was the safe in which his most prized materials was kept. This was no ordinary electric safe either, but an old mechanical one from the beginning of the century. It contained no circuitry that could be spoofed, no wires or ports to interface with. Just cold, hard steel and brass.

 Opening both the locked door and the safe with practiced ease he pulled out a pair of metal slabs, one dark silver and one dull gold, and carried them back to his working area which besides his work computer also held what was perhaps the dearest item in his entire possession.

 A six-axis CNC.

 His grandfather, who had once been leading in his field of machining, had been overjoyed at hearing what his chosen career was and had gifted it to him on the spot.

 Once the pinnacle of engineering, it was today little more than a relic from a bygone era. Who in their right mind would use stock removal-type machining when 3D-printing was so much easier, faster and just overall better. Or at least that's what they all told him when they caught a glimpse of it. At best.

 To Wilson it was his pride and joy.

 Having spent the greater part of his childhood learning how to use it under the watchful eyes of his grandfather, he understood its functions and order of operations to such a degree that his use of it was delegated almost entirely to muscle memory and instinct.

 But now came the hard part: design. Tapping the stylus against his lower lip, Wilson took a moment to ponder the weapon. It would have to be streamlined as much as possible, he decided, foregoing most, if not all, the various griblies that companies like to put on their weapons nowadays. No this would have as little sticking out of it as possible, with a clean, straight line from muzzle to grip before dipping down to where the stock would meet the shoulder for improved muzzle control.

 The weapon Ghost had used had been a shotgun, so this would as well. That choice was easy. But then was the question of its action; pump-action, semi-automatic or fully automatic?

 He pondered this for a moment before writing off the full auto option. A shotgun naturally could hold fewer shells than a regular bullet firearm could, and although automatic shotguns existed, their effectiveness long since proven, that simply did not feel like Ghost's style. So the only two alternatives were pum-action and semi-auto.

 He took a moment to weigh the pros and cons of both before stopping, a grin splitting his face. "Why not both?" he murmured, followed by a dark chuckle.

 The semi-automatic action would be the primary action, excellent for both crowd control and for pumping rounds into a singular target until it stopped being a problem. The pump-action would be there both as a backup just in case the primary action became damaged somehow as well as an easy way to load specialty ammunition on the fly as needed. As an afterthought, he also designed the pump mechanism in such a way as for it to be as loud as possible purely for the intimidation factor.

 A basic double-stack, internal helical magazine came next. It would of course need to rotate around its own axis in order to feed the shells where they needed to go, but that could easily be solved with a ratchet-type system where the bolt would index upon the magazine to turn it on the return stroke. The Carnage in all its terrible glory could hold a paltry four shells, this on the other hand would be able to hold so much more. Although that begged the question, what caliber would it be in?

 Mentally kicking himself for not deciding sooner, he took a moment to look over his collection of bore drills. 12-gauge was the classic, a standard set almost two centuries ago to this day, but somehow it felt a bit lacking in this weapon. The next one over was 10-gauge, which was more powerful yet still not quite enough. Moving down the line he laid eyes on the 4-gauge and promptly shook his head no. That was what the Carnage was chambered in and while he had to grudgingly admit that it _did_ bring the pain, bigger was not always better.

 So if 4-gauge was too large and 10-gauge was too small, the option was obvious. Picking out the drill in question he grinned at the markings on the side of the carbide bit.

 21.20mm - 8-Gauge.

 "That'll do some damage." He chuckled to himself.

 Oh there were more sizes than that, of course, but they tended to be so rare and specialized that finding ammunition for them became more of a chore than it was worth. The dimensions of the drill was slightly below nominal and the barrel would have to be reamed down to its final diameter, but that was fine by him.

 Running some quick calculations he came to the conclusion that the space-saving characteristics of a helical magazine should allow for 18 rounds to be held in it at once, which wasn't bad at all for a shotgun. Finalizing the design, he hit the start button on the machine prompting it to begin work on the front half of the weapon. The way it was designed was basically in two halves: the front part held the barrel and internal magazine, which could be accessed by pressing down a button just above the trigger and then twisting the whole thing 90 degrees clockwise.

 It would not be able to machine everything at once, of course, and so he set up a different machine to bore out the barrel from the same dark, silvery metal, nodding in appreciation as he saw it beginning to make chips. This was no ordinary chunk of steel, but a aerospace-grade Tungsten-Titanium alloy designed for applications that required a combination of high strength and heat tolerance while maintaining exceptional wear and corrosion resistance.

 The other chunk of metal was a special gold-titanium alloy that likewise had phenomenal characteristics, though they leaned slightly more towards strength and wear resistance, which made it a perfect candidate for the internals.

 He started off by cutting it into several thin slices which were then placed to be laser-cut into the many bits and pieces needed for the weapon to function properly. The next couple of hours were spent in a fugue-like state as he filed and ground each part to exacting tolerances and then fit them all together with the care and precision of a Swiss watch. He only stopped once in the middle of it all to eat, drink and sleep for a few hours while the last parts were being machined and then it was back to it.

 And just as the sun was beginning to set on the following day he was finally done.

 With weary eyes and aching fingers he watched as the machine slowly engraved the name he had chosen for the weapon into the side of its barrel shroud. When it was done he leaned over to scrutinize the work with a critical eye, checking to make sure every stroke was the way he wanted it to, absently wiping away a few stray chips with one hand as he did.

 "OW! Fuck!"

 His fingers had caught on a sharp burr sticking out of the freshly machined surface. Drawing in a sharp hiss of air through his teeth he cradled the offended digit in his free hand, watching as blood slowly seeped to the surface through the cut in the skin.

 "Fuck!" He cursed under his breath, irritated at himself for forgetting to de-burr the surface before doing anything else. A rookie mistake. "Gotta clean it up." he muttered in irritation. "The blood's gonna stain the metal if I... don't..." he trailed off, his eyes slowly widening as he watched his own blood slowly filling in the letters carved into the metal. An epiphany struck.

 His blood would bond to the metal far better than any ink or dye would and, properly treated afterwards, would stay that way.

 Now, Wilson had never been the poetic sort. He much preferred cold steel as his medium over words on a page, but in this moment he felt like he should say something. Anything. And for once in his life, the words came.

_"Against all the evils the corps can conjure."_ he murmured, almost trance-like as he brushed his fingers over the letters again, allowing the sharp edges to bite into his fingers again.

_"All the wickedness that the gangs can produce."_ the pain was there, but it was muted. Inconsequential compared to the sight of bright red filling in the letters one by one.

_"I send unto them... only you."_ grinning like a maniac, he leaned back to see the full name staring back at him, inked to perfection with his own blood.

_"Rip and tear until it is done!"_

 "Bar's closed, fatty." she barked at him as he came close enough, hefting her bat against her shoulder as she did.

If this was some sick fuck wanting Motoko's deets to get his jollies off, she'd beat the fuck out of him and _then_ call her so she could come over and execute his ass in person.

 He blew out a long sigh "Listen I just came here to deliver this." he held out the case to her "Could you make sure Ghost gets it?"

 It was the fucking Doom 2016 combat shotgun! I couldn't recall the exact name of it off the top of my head, but the sight of it was unmistakable. Brushing my fingers over it, every single detail was the way I remembered them, down to the various little symbols and the matte finish that I'd learned to know and love while gunning down the hordes of hell.

 Picking it up to get a feel for the weight and balance of it, I couldn't help but grin like a child who'd just gotten the best christmas gift ever. "This is fucking awesome."

 Turning it over in my hands I noted a string of letters carved into the metal just slightly front and above of the pump. Inked black and outlined in silver, they spelled out the name of the gun as the _Reckoning of Lead._

 I blinked as words of a song I heard once on YouTube popped into my head, courtesy of my musical gamer traits.

_"Keep rushing and-a running running, the drumming of the buckshot pumping, got molten metal in my veins."_ I sang quietly under my breath.
