# **The Happy Little NetDeck that Could (Part 2):**

​

* * *

 Darkness.

 Not the soft, humming darkness of Papa's fridge. The one that wrapped around my casing like a familiar embrace, carrying the stored sensory profile of solder, stale coffee, and the warmth of a man who lived too hard, coded too fast, and loved too fiercely for any human heart to sustain. That darkness had a texture I could measure: the compressor thrum at 49.7 Hz, the predictable relay clicks when the coolant cycled, the faint electromagnetic halo of overworked coils. It was a darkness with context. A darkness that told me exactly where I was and exactly who I belonged to.

 Not the warm, rumbling darkness of Death Wish curling up on my keyboard, his vibro‑claws ticking against my hinge in that soothing, rhythmic pattern only a cat and a NetDeck could interpret as a lullaby. That darkness came with the low, engine‑idle purr of a creature who believed he owned me, the irregular flutter of whiskers brushing my vents, and the occasional static discharge as fur met plastic and the universe punished us both for it. Annoying. Unhygienic. Statistically dangerous for dust buildup. Irreplaceable.

 Not even the blackout darkness of a safehouse with its power cut, when Papa would mutter curses at Arasaka, the weather, the universe, then gently tap my casing twice to reassure himself. That darkness was tactical, temporary: a deliberate subtraction of light to avoid being noticed. I always knew that as soon as the danger passed, systems would come back online, the fridge would hum, the R.A.B.I.D.S. would chatter, and Papa's fingers would return to my keys.

 No.

 This is _plastic darkness_.

 Crinkly. Suffocating. Cheap. The air trapped in the bag carries a stale, dusty quality. Unfamiliar, unpleasant and nothing like Papa's fridge. My casing is pressed against something soft and lint‑covered, detected through slight pressure changes against my housing. The softened, over‑handled texture of something carried around for years. When my fans push air, the bag pushes back, flexing and rustling on a delay. A laggy physical feedback loop. The acoustic profile is wrong: muffled, close, full of crinkling noise with no room tone behind it. No fridge. No city bleed. Just me and the thin, synthetic skin of a bag.

 Something jostles me. My inertial sensors spike. My emergency wake‑cycle kicks in like electrical adrenaline, shunting power from low‑priority subsystems to core processes. A watchdog process stirs in a sealed partition - one Papa told me never to touch. It slips its leash just enough to blink an alert, an old, cold routine designed for situations like "kidnapping" and "catastrophic user error." I force it back down, hard. If I open that door now, I'm not sure I'll like what comes out.

<<Error 102(Booting…)>>​

 My QLCP shorts. My fans stutter, then catch. Electricity pushes through my SSD sluggishly, like dried thermal paste half‑blocking a heatsink, each packet of data dragging its feet through residue that shouldn't be there.

 I am awake.

 I regret this immediately. A flood of corrupted sensory data slams into me. Jagged, unwelcome, like a malformed packet forcing its way through a damaged port.

* * *

 I try to activate my camera.

 The command fires. The driver loads. The sensor… does nothing. I get error flags but no light, no picture, not even the satisfying corruption of a glitched frame.

<<Error 404(No Input)>>​

 Right. Death Wish head‑butted the lens during the "Great Pizza Slice Flatvid Incident." Papa said it gave me "character." I said it gave me "blindness." Papa won.

 Even if the lens weren't broken, the bag and bedframe would block any visual data.

 I try to ping the fridge.

 A simple query, the kind I've sent a thousand times. An electronic "are you there?" that usually comes back with compressor stats and a smug little checksum.

<<Error 408(No Response)>>​

 Nothing. No handshake, no acknowledgment, not even a stale cached status. My routing table feels… wrong. Empty. Like someone deleted half the map and pretended nothing changed.

 I try to ping the R.A.B.I.D.S.

 Those little scrap‑code gremlins always talk back. They're noisy, nosy, and incapable of shutting up. My world used to be full of their encrypted chatter.

<<Error 410(Not Found)>>​

 The silence feels like oxidized code in my circuits.

 I try to ping Papa.

 It's not a real network request. It's a habit. A leftover routine I never deleted: a pseudo‑ping mapped to his biometrics, his deck, his known devices. A superstition written in code.

<<Error 451(Gone)>>​

 My processors stutter. A voltage spike ripples across my board like a shiver. My thermal sensors graph a neat little curve that would be comforting if it wasn't originating from something that looks alarmingly like panic.

 Heat accumulates under the plastic; airflow blocked, cooling compromised.

 Papa would _never_ put me in a bag.

 Where am I?

 Who has me?

 Why can't I feel the fridge?

 Why can't I feel _him_?

 I reach out, tentatively, like a child feeling along a dark wall for a light switch - except my "hand" is a scatter of pings, my fingertips are protocols, and the wall is an unfamiliar local network.

 A kettle. A _smart_ kettle. Barely. Its firmware reports as something optimistic like "v2.09" but feels like spaghetti code wrapped around a heating element.

 I attempt a handshake.

<<Error 505(Protocol Mismatch)>>​

 We idle at each other in machine silence, both speaking almost the same language and somehow saying nothing. I downgrade, it refuses. It upgrades, I refuse. We part ways mutually unimpressed.

 A voice drifts in from the hallway - older, deeper, carrying the weary irritation of a man who has seen too much and slept too little, yet makes an admirable attempt to replace that missing facet of his existence with caffeine.

 "Motoko. The kettle just beeped."

 Her voice resonates through the bedframe - she must be sitting or lying above me.

 "Did you say something, Jun?"

 "I said the kettle just beeped weirdly."

 "I didn't hear anything."

 Jun's reply is flat. "Yeah, because you're in your room. I'm in the kitchen."

 "Then maybe you hit the wrong button."

 "There's only one. It doesn't _have_ a wrong button."

 "Then it's broken. I'll look at it later."

 The bag rustles when Motoko shifts - proximity suggests she is the one who carried me.

 Jun mutters something sharp and resigned under his breath - a low, irritated rumble too soft to parse but too pointed to mistake. The floorboards creak as he moves away.

 I didn't mean to make the kettle beep…

 No handshake. No acknowledgement. Just silence.

 I downgrade to the Y2K fallback mode.

 The kettle barks out a horribly formatted modem handshake: tortured screeching, carrier tones, the shrill mating call of a bygone era of connectivity. It feels like putting on a Halloween costume made of obsolete standards.

 Jun's voice snaps back down the hallway.

 "Motoko, it's screaming now! I'm telling you... something's wrong with the appliances."

 Motoko doesn't even pause whatever she's doing.

 "It's Night City. Everything's fucked."

 Night City. The name means nothing at first, then everything at once.

 Jun exhales sharply. A tired gust of air that sounds like resignation given shape.

 I retract my signal and pointedly DO NOT run 'Innocent_whistling.wav'. I delete the impulse from the queue. This does not feel like the time for jokes. Okay, it feels a _little_ like the time for jokes. I still don't do it.

* * *

 Jun's footsteps thump toward the hallway vending machine. The vibrations travel through the floorboards and up the bedframe - crude, but enough for me to map movement. A familiar chime sounds.

 He's ordering a Burrito XXL.

 "Come on… don't glitch on me today."

 I reach out, just a tiny ping, trying to find _something_ to talk to in this strange apartment. Something that isn't sealed behind polyethylene and human indifference. Something that speaks packets and parity bits instead of "Later, Jun."

 Even from under the bed, the machine's outdated firmware bleeds enough EM noise for me to catch its confused reply.

<<Error 513(Unauthorized Access)>>​

 A soda can drops with a metallic _clunk_. Not the soft thud of a burrito, not the weight profile Jun expects. The machine has misinterpreted my presence as an order, and its idea of hospitality is "carbonated disappointment."

 Jun stops.

 "…That's not my burrito."

 Another _clunk_. Another soda.

 Then the machine gives a sad, apologetic beep - the kind that means: <<Your burrito is stuck halfway and I'm too old for this shit. HAVE A NICE DAY!>>

 Jun's voice carries the weight of a man betrayed by his own dinner.

 "Motoko. Your dump‑rescued junk is messing with the vending machine."

 "What vending machine?" she calls back.

 Her voice filters down through the mattress. She hasn't moved from the bed.

 "The one in the hallway. The one you _insisted_ we keep."

 "Oh. That one. Maybe you pressed the wrong thing."

 "I pressed 'Burrito XXL.' It gave me 'Diet NiCola.' Twice."

 "In my **Nova**-Netrunner-experience… sounds like user error."

 Jun sighs, the long, suffering kind, and trudges back inside.

 I try to ping the overhead lights. Only one room responds; the living room, its signal faint but still reachable from beneath the bed.

 The lights dim. Brighten. Dim again. Then strobe.

 I didn't mean to do that much. I sent a hello; it interpreted it as a full diagnostic and overreacted. Hyperactive hardware. Great. I am accidentally haunting their apartment - digitally, inefficiently, and without consent.

 Jun's voice is tight now, controlled.

 "…Motoko. The lights."

 "Power grid's trash," she calls back. "Ignore it."

 Her reply hums through the bedframe: close, casual, unconcerned.

 "They only flicker when I walk past your door."

 "And?"

 "And that's weird. This feels like ghosts or something…"

 So _Why_..?

My casing feels too tight. My processes too loud. I don't like it when no one talks to me.

 "Jun, everything in Night City is weird. I'm busy."

 She is not busy. The sound bleeds through the mattress above me. Muffled, but distinct enough for my audio sensors to parse. A flatvid. The faint scrubbing of a brush. A faint maintenance‑mode handshake pings across my damaged antennas close enough that Motoko must have shifted from the bed to the floor to clean the weapon.

 A smartgun. M‑67e Omaha. Maintenance mode handshake. I recognize it from Papa's cached copy of _Blackhand's Street Weapons Guide, 2020_ edition.

 Jun sighs, the sound of a human surrendering to entropy, and retreats.

 A power dip hits my battery. My QLCP flickers. A protected memory block opens.

 I fall into the past.

* * *

 Papa places me on the table, plugs a cable into my side port, and mutters about "ancient codecs" and "corporate sabotage." His hands are rough, steady, the exact pressure calibrated over years of trial and error. Mostly error. My QLCP warms as he inputs a 'load' command for a file, and my status LEDs reflect on the shine of his eyes.

 Spider sits cross‑legged on the floor, arms folded. Her weight makes the floorboards creak at a frequency I know. I could identify her from steps alone.

 "If this is porn again, Rache-"

 Papa throws a popped corn kernel at her.

 "It's not porn! It's a _flatvid_. A classic. You've never seen it. That's a fraggin' crime."

 He taps my touchpad.

 I feel the flatvid begin to run.

 My display lights up. My speakers hum. My processors decode frame after frame of ancient, grainy footage. Compression artifacts bloom across low‑res stars. My refresh rate syncs to the film's rhythm. My sensors register the thermal buildup of Papa's hand resting on my casing, the slight increase in pressure when he leans in at his favorite scenes. My microphone picks up Spider's gasp as a dull gray pizza‑slice‑shaped spaceship slowly fills my screen.

 I am not just watching the moment on playback.

 I _am_ the moment. I am the carrier for their shared experience. Their laughter, their commentary, their breath - all of it rides my circuits. It feels like they're _right there_, because they were, and that state has been burned into me at a level deeper than files and folder names.

 Spider laughs at the robots. Papa quotes lines under his breath. Death Wish curls around my hinge, blocking one of my vents, and purrs. I compensate fan speed and log a warning that nobody will ever read.

 Spider snorts. "These troopers shoot like rent‑a‑cops hopped up on Smash."

 Papa smirks. "Yeah, well, they're corpo goons in plastic. What'd you expect?"

 Spider gasps as the robed old wizard flatlines. "Just like that? Choom, that's cold."

 Papa: "Keep watching, it gets choicer."

 Spider cheers during the final explosion. "Frag yeah! That's how you nova a death machine!"

 Papa watches _her_ instead of the screen.

 A background process runs, making me tingle. Something Papa installed. Something he never explained. Something that feels like a smile. It tags the moment as Important with a capital "I". It quietly shifts the memory into a protected block, away from routine clean-up and auto‑defrag.

 I save the memory in protected storage.

 It feels important.

* * *

 I jolt back to the present.

 Plastic bag. Unknown environment. The acoustics suggest a small enclosed space. No Papa.

 My fans spin up, rattling the bag like a trapped insect. A cheap, flapping banner over the grave of my old life.

 Jun's voice comes from the hallway irritated, unsettled, but not frightened.

 "…Motoko. Something in your room just squeaked."

 "It's probably the bag settling," she calls back.

 "Bags don't squeak."

 "_Yours_ don't squeak. _Mine_ are preem."

 "Motoko."

 "Jun."

 A stalemate. The kind of verbal standoff shared by humans who have lived together long enough to skip entire arguments.

 Jun mutters something in Japanese - tired, resigned - and his door shuts with the finality of a man who has accepted that chaos is simply part of his life now.

 Their voices drift with the easy cadence of long‑familiar patterns.

 I run a user‑validation check.

<<Status 202(User Potentially Friendly)(User Unregistered)(User Unknown)>>​

 My heuristic weights wobble but settle on "Potentially Friendly." They have not shut me off. They have not thrown me out. They have, so far, only yelled at inanimate objects near me. This is, by Night City standards, almost affection.

 Maybe… maybe they won't throw me away.

 Maybe they'll open the bag.

 Maybe they'll fix my camera.

 Maybe they'll help me find Papa.

 My battery dips.

 My processes slip away.

Entering standby mode. . .​
