Motoko and the TC from an Outsider's perspective: A speculative essay

 So, who or what is Motoko to the TC from anybody that isn't in deep denial?

 First, she is a legacy. Now this can be good or bad. The exact responsibilities and protection that this offers varies wildly from culture to culture, boss to boss, and depends heavily on the person that the legacy is from. Sometimes it's good, other times it's bad. This is mostly irrelevant now though, as she has moved beyond this status. It does provide context though, in that she would already be considered a part of their sphere of influence.

 Second, she is not going to be considered an active member. She doesn't send anything up, she doesn't attend most of the meet ups, she doesn't wear the colors. She is disrespectful enough that if she were a true member, she would probably have been "dealt with" by now. She also tends to wander off and do her own thing a LOT which would be frowned on by the TC as a member.

 So where does that leave her?

 Neither Fish nor Fowl

 As I said earlier, she's "the guy". There is no commonly accepted formal name for this person. They're not even associates. They are personal connections and resources of a given leader or clique within the hierarchy. And this can be near anyone. A pig farmer that disposes of bodies. A crooked car dealer that can give you a car with no identifying marks. The doctor willing to stitch someone up with no questions asked. Or even more innocent relationships. The priest that can set a boy right by his soul. A barber that gets the haircut just right. It doesn't really matter, just that they're useful to that person or group.

 Which is the key to understanding everything else I'm going to say: They are useful to that person or group _within the gang_ and not _part_ of the gang.

 This both gives them protection and a target on their back. On the one hand, the person that they are connected to, usually a boss but sometimes not, has to protect them against threats. To not do so is to be seen as weak or incompetent. That both encourages enemies and demoralizes allies. On the other, they are not really gang resources. They are personal. Which means that they are fair game when a civil war starts to break out. Where shooting up a gang associate will get you targeted by the rest of the gang for hurting their interests, well, this kind of "guy" getting intimidated, beaten, driven out, or murdered isn't really the rest of the gang's problem. It's personal. It drives the isolation of the target and reduces their resources to respond to that or future attacks.

 There is a reason that intergang warfare is usually started by soldiers or street rats fighting while intragang warfare is usually heralded by someone's friend or lover dying.

 Now, Motoko is unusual in that usually the "guy" is only connected in one way to the gang, where she has, by my count, four separate connections. Jun, Wakako, Fujimura, and a relatively weak one from the unnamed TC boss she saved. That lends her a level of protection, as anyone choosing to target one of her connections would have to be concerned about potentially pissing off the rest of her network. It also doesn't hurt that she has some pretty solid connections to many gangs in Night City, from the Mox to the Netrunners to the Nomads. Attacking her is a bit of a risky proposition really.

 All of this sounds ideal, but there is a further wrinkle in the issue. She's an independent combat specialist. Now this usually doesn't happen for a variety of reasons. First, most gangs start as some form of defense organization. Whether a neighborhood watch or a loose collection of dealers protecting their trade, very few try to be a gang from the beginning. That in mind, an organization defined by specializing in combat bringing in an independent is just strange. A bit like a plumber bringing in another plumber from a different company. Is the work too much? Are you all overbooked? Are you just incompetent? Not usually questions a gang wants to be asked you know. Most people asking those questions do so with bullets after all!

 The second reason that "the guy" being a combat specialist is rare is the implication. If a subordinate is calling in his own "guy" or really even has a "guy" for that, the boss has to question why. Who is this subordinate targeting or worried about that he isn't willing to come to the family for help? There's one really obvious answer; one that gets the bosses shoulder blades itching. And if it is a boss that has this type of "guy" his subordinates have to consider why he's going to outsiders for this. Does he not believe in them? Does he think they're targeting him? Are they about to be "retired"?

 Finally, there's the fact that you just can't use them for all that much. If they're super active, they're probably wanting into the family, which clarifies their position. Or gets them shot. It's honestly fairly disruptive overall.

 Which brings us to disruption personified, Motoko. There's actually a lot of subtlety in her interactions with the TC. One major incident would be the borg assassination after the party. To the TC boss she saved, she looked like a legacy streetrat wannabe that had just showed an excellent resume for a position in the TC. He likely genuinely thought he was being nice by offering her a position until Fujimura shut him down by basically calling her his "guy" (and effectively adopting her). 

 As an amusing side note, the TC boss had the Doll as one of his "guys", effectively serving as a way to show off his wealth, power, and magnanimity. Jun bringing his kid sister along with her already borged arms and cave goblin mannerisms would have looked essentially the same. Meaning that until she offed the borg, well, she was on the same social level as the Doll to the bosses. ...it's probably a good thing that she kept her mouth full of sushi all night.

 Now, all that in mind, what does this scene say to me?

 First, her entrance sent some mixed signals. No colors but with a high ranking member? Probably arm candy. The "weird looks" were probably a lot of people wondering if the Oni had such young tastes, especially since I can't imagine his tryst with Akari is particularly unknown. Everyone likes gossiping about the boss after all! Then she ends up being pulled away from Jun and set off on her own. One way of interpreting this is that she was being trained personally. The OTHER interpretation is that she is a distraction for Jun that Fujimura took the first available excuse to get rid of. A wise person would shrug, figure the coin flip wasn't worth the trouble and move on. 

 Wisdom is not a stat in Cyberpunk.

 Some guys decided to poke the button. They weren't told to stop, which is good for them, but their leader started losing to a girl smaller than him, which is bad. He signals to his friend to help him, his friend gets smashed into paste in a blink and Motoko is about to lay back into him. And _that_ is when Jun intervenes. See, here's the thing. His lesson was NOT that she was not to be fought. Fight the goblin all you want, she's fine. He actually apologizes for her poor manners (She never introduced herself). But if you jump her and try to gang up on her like she's some sort of outsider, he's going to beat you to death with your own spine.

HIS SISTER IS NOT AN OUTSIDER TO BE FUCKED WITH.

 It was actually very neatly done, showing her bona fides in combat, her backing in the gang, and her independence from the more traditional hierarchy. 

 Which in a final note, likely reflects how the TC bosses were interacting with her. They found a strange and curious thing, started poking at it, and got startled by the response, both in its intensity and in the direction it came from. A bunch of cats wondering if the new thing was fish or fowl, only to find out it was neither. It was a Roomba bouncing off of walls and ignoring them entirely as it went about its daily business.
